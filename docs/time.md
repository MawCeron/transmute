# Time

Time conversions use the second (s) as the SI base unit.

## Sub-Second

Nanoseconds (ns), microseconds (us), and milliseconds (ms) cover the range from computer clock cycles to human reaction times. Network latency is typically measured in milliseconds; CPU operations in nanoseconds. The shake (10 ns) is informal nuclear physics slang for the time scale of fission chain reactions.

## Standard

The second is defined by the cesium-133 hyperfine transition. Minutes (min), hours (h), days (d), and weeks (w) are familiar calendar units. The Julian year (y) used here is 365.25 days, accounting for leap years.

## Calendar

The average month (mo) is 365.25/12 days ≈ 30.44 days — an approximation since calendar months vary. Decade, century, and millennium follow from the Julian year. The eon, representing one billion years, is used in geology and cosmology.

## Physics

The Planck time (planck, about 5.39×10⁻⁴⁴ s) is the smallest meaningful time interval in current physics — below this scale, our models of spacetime break down.

## Practical

A fortnight (1,209,600 s) is two weeks, common in British English and some financial contexts. Some languages and cultures count time in units that map awkwardly onto standard ones — a dog year, for instance, is sometimes cited as roughly seven human years, giving about 220 million seconds per "year" in canine experience. A short nap of around 20 minutes (1200 s) is well documented as an effective rest interval in sleep research.
