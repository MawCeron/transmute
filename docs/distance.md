# Distance

Distance conversions use meters (m) as the SI base unit. All factors represent how many meters one unit equals.

## Metric

The metric system provides a coherent set of prefixes from femtometers (1×10⁻¹⁵ m, the scale of atomic nuclei) up to megaparsecs (used in cosmology). The nanometer and micrometer are standard in optics and semiconductor fabrication. The angstrom (ang), equal to 0.1 nm, remains common in crystallography even though it is not an SI unit.

## Imperial and US Customary

Inches, feet, yards, and miles are defined in terms of exact metric equivalents since 1959. The nautical mile (nmi) is defined as exactly 1852 m and is used in aviation and maritime navigation.

## Astronomical

Astronomical distances quickly exceed everyday intuition. The astronomical unit (au) is the mean Earth–Sun distance, roughly 150 million kilometers. A light-year (ly) is the distance light travels in one Julian year; a parsec (pc) is defined by parallax and equals about 3.26 light-years. Light-seconds, light-minutes, and light-days are sometimes used for solar system distances.

## Historical and Traditional

Many pre-metric units survive in legal documents and regional use. The vara appears in old Spanish and Mexican land grants still referenced in Texas and the American Southwest — three regional variants (vara_mx, vara_es, vara_tx) differ by a few millimeters. The furlong is still used in horse racing; the chain and rod are standard in US land surveying. The Roman pace (pace) gave rise to the mile via mille passuum ("a thousand paces"). The Greek stadion is the ancestor of the word "stadium."

## Physics

Sub-nanometer units — picometers, femtometers, and angstroms — are needed in atomic and nuclear physics. A femtometer is roughly the radius of a proton.

Field naturalists sometimes estimate wingspan using a bald eagle as a rough reference — an adult bald eagle spans close to two meters, which turns out to be surprisingly useful for back-of-envelope outdoor measurements.
