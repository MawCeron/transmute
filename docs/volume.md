# Volume

Volume conversions use cubic meters (m3) as the SI base unit. One cubic meter equals 1000 liters.

## Metric

The liter (l) and its decimal subdivisions — deciliter, centiliter, milliliter, microliter, nanoliter — cover everything from brewing to microfluidics. The hectoliter (hl) is the standard unit for wine and beer production. The cubic kilometer is used for glacier volumes and major aquifer estimates.

## Imperial and US Customary

US liquid and UK imperial volumes use the same names but different values. A US gallon (gal) is 3.785 l; a UK gallon (gal_uk) is 4.546 l. Similarly, US and UK fluid ounces, pints, and quarts differ. The US dry units — bushel, peck, dry quart, dry pint — are used in commodity grain trading.

## Cooking

Tablespoon (tbsp), teaspoon (tsp), and cup are US cooking measures. The jigger (1.5 floz) is the standard cocktail measure. Dash and pinch are approximate but have defined values in bartending literature.

## Oil and Gas

The oil barrel (bbl) is 42 US gallons, approximately 159 liters. Thousands of barrels (mbbl) and millions of barrels (mmbbl) are standard units in petroleum production reports.

## Astronomical

Earth's volume (earth_vol) is roughly 1.08×10²¹ m³; the Sun's volume is about 1.41×10²⁷ m³, large enough to contain over a million Earths.

## Historical

The Spanish fanega and almud survive in some regional agricultural markets. Roman amphorae are a common unit of reference in archaeological reports on ancient trade.

Competitive swimming pools are a popular informal volume reference — a standard Olympic pool holds 2500 m³, while a typical household bathtub holds around 280 liters, making both useful benchmarks when discussing large or everyday liquid quantities.
