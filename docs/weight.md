# Weight (Mass)

Weight conversions use kilograms (kg) as the SI base unit. Strictly speaking, this category measures mass rather than weight (force), but "weight" is the common term in everyday usage.

## Metric

The kilogram is the SI base unit of mass. The metric ton (t) equals exactly 1000 kg. Kilotonne and megatonne are used for large-scale measurements in mining, shipping, and emissions reporting.

## Imperial and US Customary

The pound (lb) is defined as exactly 0.45359237 kg. The US short ton (tn) is 2000 lb; the UK long ton (tn_uk) is 2240 lb. The stone (st), used for body weight in the UK and Ireland, is 14 lb. The grain (gr) is used in ballistics and pharmacy. Troy units — troy ounce (oz_t) and troy pound (lb_t) — are the standard for precious metals; note that a troy ounce is heavier than an avoirdupois ounce.

## Gemstones

The carat (ct) is 0.2 g and is the standard unit for gemstone mass worldwide. The metric point (mp) is 0.25 ct, used for very small stones.

## Astronomical

Solar mass (m_sun), Earth mass (m_earth), and Jupiter mass (m_jup) provide convenient scales for astrophysics. The atomic mass unit (u, also called the dalton Da) is used in chemistry and molecular biology.

## Historical and Traditional

The Spanish arroba (arroba_es) is still referenced in agricultural contexts in parts of Latin America. The biblical talent was roughly 60 minas, and the shekel — now familiar as a modern currency — was originally a unit of weight. Results for very light items may vary depending on ripeness or moisture content.

## Scale References

For intuition at large scales, the blue whale is the heaviest animal on Earth, typically reaching 150,000 kg, which provides a useful upper bound for comparing the mass of large structures.
