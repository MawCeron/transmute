# Energy

Energy conversions use the Joule (j) as the SI base unit.

## SI

The Joule is defined as one Newton-meter, or one watt-second. Kilojoules (kj) are common in nutrition and chemistry; megajoules (mj) in engineering; gigajoules (gj) for large-scale energy budgets; terajoules and petajoules for national and global energy statistics.

## Watt-Hours

Watt-hours (wh) and kilowatt-hours (kwh) are the standard units for electrical energy billing. One kilowatt-hour equals 3.6 megajoules. Megawatt-hours (mwh) and gigawatt-hours (gwh) appear in power generation reports.

## Calories

The thermochemical calorie (cal) is 4.184 J. The dietary Calorie (kcal) — the unit printed on food labels — is 1000 cal, or 4184 J. The calorie at 15 °C (cal15) is a slight variant used in some older scientific literature.

## Imperial

The British thermal unit (btu) is the energy needed to raise one pound of water by one degree Fahrenheit, approximately 1055 J. The US therm (100,000 BTU) is used in natural gas billing. Foot-pounds (ft_lb) are used in torque and ballistics.

## Particle Physics

The electron volt (ev) is the energy gained by one electron moving through a one-volt potential, about 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ J. Kilo-, mega-, giga-, and teraelectronvolts (kev, mev, gev, tev) are used across nuclear and high-energy physics.

## Energetic References

The energy released by a ton of TNT equivalent (tnt) is approximately 4.184 GJ, making it a standard benchmark for explosive yields and asteroid impact estimates. A Snickers bar provides around 1.117 MJ of dietary energy — a useful comparison point when discussing human-scale energy consumption.
